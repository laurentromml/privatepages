01 — Executive Summary

The business, the value, and the recommendation

Auroraflow LLC is a five-year-old wellness center in Durham, North Carolina, organized in July 2021 with its operating location opening in December 2022. The business serves the Triangle market with massage, aesthetician services, and retail products, operating on the Mango Mint POS/CRM platform with a team of 20+ providers. It has earned consecutive Best Spa recognition from Indyweek and Durham Magazine in 2025. Revenue has grown from $14,901 in the 2022 build year to a current annualized run rate of approximately $1.06 million, with the first profitable calendar year achieved in 2025 ($24,110 of net income). Growth has plateaued in 2026, and trailing-twelve-month profitability has reverted to a slight loss as operating costs have outpaced revenue scaling.

The business is held 51% by Castle M. Frame (Founder, primary operator) and 49% by Lauren R. McCullough (Founder and COO, strategic role). Castle is voluntarily exiting on amicable terms. The withdrawal proceeds with Lauren's consent under Operating Agreement §8.1, outside the appraisal mechanism of Article IX, providing the parties full flexibility on price and structure.

Valuation Conclusion
Fair Market Value: $0

Derived from a multi-method valuation framework applying three IRS-recognized approaches — income (DCF), market (comparable transactions), and asset (net asset value). All three methodologies converge on the same result. Castle's 51% economic interest in the equity is, mathematically and defensibly, $0.

The DCF yields negligible enterprise value once the operator role is replaced with market-rate labor; the market-multiple approach is constrained by the business's modest profitability; and the asset approach reflects deeply negative book equity (approximately -$202,585) once the previously off-books gift card obligation ($94,173) and accurate credit card balances are properly reflected. Standard 409A-style discounts for lack of marketability and key person dependence only floor the value further.

Recommended buyout

Given the zero-equity reality, the buyout cannot be structured as a payment for equity. The recommended package is $30,000 in goodwill compensation recognizing five years of founder contribution, two Best Spa awards earned under Castle's leadership, and the 20+ provider team she built, plus continued transition compensation totaling $12,000–$18,000 covering her August–September full-time period and a six-month consulting retainer. Total cash to Castle is approximately $45,000–$51,000 over five years. Castle additionally receives release from personal guarantees on business debt and credit cards, worth approximately $50,000–$80,000 in risk relief. All compensation is contingent on her continued engagement — full-time operational responsibility through legal close (August–September 2026), then approximately 10–20 hours per month of knowledge transfer and on-call availability during the consulting period (October 2026 – March 2027) — with forfeiture triggered by material breach of these obligations or the restrictive covenants.

The $30,000 goodwill payment is structured as $10,000 upfront at financial completion (March 31, 2027) plus $20,000 on a 48-month promissory note at 8% interest (approximately $488 per month, with no prepayment provision). The upfront amount is funded from a cash-out refinance of the existing institutional debt (Business Installment Loan + Xelle Ventures Loan, totaling $99,322); the monthly note payments are absorbed from business operating cash flow over a four-year amortization period.

The transaction is structured as an LLC redemption under OA §3.3(g) — the business buys back Castle's interest, with Lauren becoming 100% sole member by operation of the redemption. The refinance pays off the existing institutional debt that Castle currently guarantees, releasing her cleanly as a natural consequence of the loan payoff. Both the upfront payment and note payments are characterized as §736(a) guaranteed payments for services — deductible to the LLC and ordinary income to Castle. An S-Corporation election effective October 1, 2026 saves Lauren approximately $4,000–$8,000 per year in self-employment tax going forward.

Methodology convergence

Implied enterprise value by method
All three methodologies converge on $0 equity FMV after discounts

How the conclusion is derived

The DCF yields negligible enterprise value once the operator role is replaced with market-rate labor (~$65,000–$75,000 loaded for a Durham-area spa GM). The market-multiple approach (SDE and EBITDA at 1.5–2.5x and 3–4x respectively) is constrained by the business's modest profitability. The asset approach reflects deeply negative book equity. Weighting the methodologies (40% income / 40% market / 20% asset) produces a pre-discount EV of approximately -$17,000.

Standard 409A-style discounts then apply: a 30–35% discount for lack of marketability (private business, restricted transferability under OA Article VIII), and a 10–15% key person discount for operational dependence on Castle. Applied to an already-negative weighted EV, the discounts only floor the value further.

Final Determination
FMV of equity = $0

Equity value cannot go below zero for valuation purposes; limited liability protects members from owing more than their contributed capital. Castle's 51% economic interest in the equity is, mathematically and defensibly, $0.

Tax-minimization mechanics

All of the following should be reviewed and refined in consultation with Heyward CPA PLLC and engaged transaction counsel.

§736(a) vs. §736(b) characterization

The $30,000 payment (both $10,000 upfront and $20,000 note) should be characterized as a §736(a) guaranteed payment for services rather than a §736(b) capital interest redemption. Under §736(a), payments are ordinary income to Castle and deductible to the LLC. Because the equity FMV is $0, there is no economic basis for §736(b) treatment.

S-Corporation election effective October 1, 2026

When Castle exits, the LLC automatically becomes a disregarded entity. Electing S-Corporation status on Form 2553 effective October 1, 2026 allows Lauren to pay herself a reasonable salary subject to FICA, with remaining profits flowing as K-1 distributions not subject to self-employment tax. Expected savings: $4,000–$8,000 per year. Form 2553 must be filed within 75 days (by mid-December 2026). Single biggest tax lever in this report

  • Capital account reconciliation — ~$51K discrepancy between K-1 sum and M-2 on 2025 return
  • Gift card liability accounting cleanup — prior period adjustment to recognize $94K liability on Schedule L
  • Timing of payments across tax years — note payments naturally spread tax recognition across 2027–2031
  • Lauren's note receivable — characterize as Loan from Partner under OA §5.2, document with promissory note and interest accrual
05 — Conditions of the Buyout

Why a redemption, and what protections are required

LLC redemption vs. cross-purchase

A traditional partner buyout could theoretically be structured as a cross-purchase (Lauren personally buys Castle's interest from her own funds) or an LLC redemption (the business buys back Castle's interest, funded by business debt). The LLC redemption is the appropriate structure here because:

  1. Lauren does not need to inject personal capital
  2. The refinance pays off existing debt that Castle is currently guaranteeing, releasing her cleanly as a natural consequence of the loan payoff
  3. It produces the cleanest tax characterization (§736(a) deductibility for the LLC)
  4. It produces the cleanest governance outcome (Lauren as sole member with full control of an amended Operating Agreement)

Under OA §3.3(g), redemption requires unanimous member consent. Castle consents, so the requirement is satisfied. Under §8.1, withdrawal requires consent of the Disinterested Member (Lauren), also satisfied. The transaction proceeds under Article VIII (Transfer with consent) rather than as a Buy-Sell Event under Article IX, providing maximum flexibility on price, structure, and terms.

Castle's continuing obligations and conditions for ongoing payments

The compensation structure recognizes that Castle is being paid for ongoing work and engagement during the transition, not for exiting alone. Compensation is contingent on her performance of the scope described below and her compliance with the conditions that follow.

Scope of work — August through September 2026 (full-time as partner)

Castle continues in her current operational role as primary day-to-day operator: managing the team, overseeing service delivery, maintaining client relationships, handling vendor and supplier interactions, and operational decision-making jointly with Lauren. She also begins transition handoff activities — documenting SOPs, introducing Lauren and successor team members to key relationships, and preparing transition documentation. Compensation flows as guaranteed payment under §707(c) at approximately her current run rate.

Scope of work — October 2026 through March 2027 (six-month consulting period)

Castle transitions to a 1099 contractor role with reduced involvement. Expected scope:

  • Approximately 10–20 hours per month average commitment
  • On-call availability for operational questions during business hours, with defined response time (e.g., 48 hours)
  • Knowledge transfer on operational, client, vendor, and provider relationships
  • Specific administrative handoff items (vendor accounts, account access, client communications regarding ownership change)
  • Strategic input where her institutional knowledge adds value

Day-to-day operational responsibility has shifted to Lauren during this period. The consulting retainer ($1,000–$2,000/month) compensates Castle for availability and the specific handoff work; she is not expected to maintain operational decision-making authority.

Conditions for the upfront payment and ongoing note payments

  1. Completion of the August–September partner period and the October–March consulting period in good faith and according to the scope described above
  2. No material breach of the restrictive covenants — including during the 48-month note amortization period
  3. Active cooperation with the refinancing process, including signing all reasonably-requested lender documents
  4. No disparagement of the business, Lauren, employees, providers, or clients
  5. Confirmation that all personal guarantees have been released, or that Castle has not impeded the release process
  6. Mutual release re-affirmed at financial completion

A material breach during the consulting period or note period triggers forfeiture of the unpaid upfront amount (if before release) and acceleration/forfeiture of the remaining note balance.

Escrow and note documentation

The $20,000 note is documented as a formal promissory note from the LLC to Castle, executed at financial completion:

  • Principal: $20,000
  • Interest rate: 8% per annum
  • Term: 48 months (April 2027 – March 2031)
  • Monthly payment: approximately $488 (level amortization)
  • No prepayment provision — note runs the full 48-month term
  • Forfeiture trigger: material breach of restrictive covenants causes acceleration and write-off of remaining balance
  • Acceleration on third-party sale of LLC (Lauren's protection if she sells the business)

Mutual general release

A mutual general release is executed at financial completion (March 31, 2027), releasing both parties from all claims arising from the operation of the business through that date. Standard carve-outs for fraud, intentional misconduct, ongoing obligations under the buyout agreement and note, and tax obligations imposed by law.

In Summary

This buyout structure honors Castle's five years of co-founding contribution to Auroraflow with a fair, defensible package, while reflecting the honest economic reality that the business carries no positive equity value. It accomplishes (a) clean ownership transition to Lauren, (b) release of Castle from personal liability exposure through the natural mechanics of the debt refinance, (c) preservation of business continuity, (d) tax-efficient treatment for both parties spread across multiple tax years, and (e) full funding from a business refinance without requiring personal capital injection from Lauren. Castle exits with predictable cash compensation over five years, release from personal guarantees, restrictive covenants she can comfortably comply with, and her independent practice intact. Lauren retains 100% of a business she can operate with full control, on a debt-restructured basis with tax-optimized treatment going forward.
