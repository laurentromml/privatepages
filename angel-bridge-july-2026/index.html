The problem

By one widely cited 2025 study, roughly 95 percent of enterprise AI pilots never reach production. The models are not the problem. The data underneath them is. You cannot run AI on a company whose most valuable knowledge lives in people's heads.

Nowhere is that gap wider than in the automotive aftermarket, a $435B channel that runs on relationships and phone calls. Frontline CRM adoption sits around 20 to 30 percent, and it's not going up. Reps walk out of shops every day knowing what's selling, what's slipping, and where the next order is hiding, and by the next stop it's gone. Managers coach off gut and stale CRM fields while the sales force that holds all the relationships is retiring. Every dropped thread is a lost deal in an industry where the relationship is the asset.

The solution

Capture what the frontline already knows, at the moment they know it, at a cost of seconds. Then turn it into signal a manager can act on and structure an AI agent can run on.

Our conviction, validated in two years on the ground: these teams don't need to be replaced, they need to do more with less. That's what customers tell us in nearly every conversation. Systems of action are already being built for software and the glamorous industries. Someone will build them for the industries that keep the physical world running, and whoever captures and structures the frontline data first becomes the layer this industry runs its AI on.

The product: Minecart

Your reps know more than your reports. A rep leaves a shop, talks into Minecart for thirty seconds, and gets on with their day. That one capture becomes a reminder for the rep and a tracked opportunity for the team. Managers see what's actually happening across their accounts and point each rep at the right person at the right time. No call reports, no forms.

How it works, and why it works

Capture is the whole game, so we made it cost nothing. A rep taps the red button, talks for thirty seconds, and gets on with their day. No forms, no call reports. The note ties itself to the account, the follow-up sets itself, and nothing the rep hears gets lost between stops.

Then it flows both ways. Managers finally see what's actually happening across every account, and their priorities land inside each rep's day: the schedule, the accounts to hit, the talking points to lead with. The rep walks in sharper. The manager stops chasing reports. And because reps see their own notes turn into wins, they capture more, which makes everyone's next call smarter.

We call it the loop: Capture → Signal → Attention → Action, circling one center, compounding execution. Every lap makes the data underneath richer, and that data is exactly what enterprise AI is missing everywhere else.

And we don't fight the systems companies already pay for. Minecart works with or without a CRM and feeds HubSpot and Salesforce in real time. For Advance Auto Parts, Snap-on, and WorldPac the pitch is simple: we complete Salesforce, we don't compete. Frontline reps who never justified a Salesforce seat get a workflow they'll actually use, and structured data flows back into the CRM the company already bought.

Where we sit

The category people know is revenue intelligence: Gong, Chorus, Clari. Strong tools, but built for one world. Inside sales reps selling software from a desk, where every conversation is already a recorded video call and everyone lives in Salesforce. That is not our world. Our buyer sells real physical products, thousands of SKUs, from the field, over the phone and across the counter, and the intelligence that matters, what is selling, what is slipping, which part a shop keeps asking for, gets said out loud and never typed. There is no video call to mine.

That is also why you cannot just point a SaaS tool at the aftermarket. Salesforce and HubSpot assume the rep logs in and updates fields, and on this front line only 20 to 30 percent do, so the company pays for the system of record and never gets the record. We capture what the field actually knows, turn it into action, and feed it back into the CRM they already bought. The data, the SKUs, and the way these people work are all different, and that difference is the moat.

Business model

Today it's a seat-based subscription that's easy to say yes to: a platform fee plus per-user pricing, landing with field sales teams. The expansion is already showing up on its own. Inside sales and customer success run the same loop, and from there it's every knowledge worker in the building. As agents take on real work, pricing shifts from seats to utility: we get paid for what the system does, not who logs in. That moves us off the IT-tools line and into the labor budget, a far bigger wallet.

Market potential

The US aftermarket is $435B and deeply fragmented; the top 50 distributors hold only about 30 percent of it. That's the beachhead. The same buyer, the same field-sales motion, and the same lost-intelligence problem repeat across tool and hardware distribution ($94B), MRO ($162B), construction supply, and ag. The endgame is the position Verisk holds in insurance and CoStar holds in real estate: the data utility an industry cannot operate without.
