The situation

Roughly 95 percent of enterprise AI pilots never reach production, even as every company bets on AI to make its people more productive. The failures, some of them very public, from horizontal chatbots to Salesforce's Agentforce, keep tracing back to the same two problems: the AI has no real context to run on, and it cannot touch the actual work. That gap is widest in industries built on relationships and hard-won expertise. You cannot run AI on a company whose most valuable knowledge lives in people's heads.

The problem

Nowhere is that gap wider than the automotive aftermarket, a $500B+ channel that still runs on field visits and phone calls. Frontline CRM adoption sits at 20 to 30 percent and is not climbing, because those systems only store data. They give the rep and the manager almost nothing back.

So the intelligence evaporates. A rep walks out of a shop knowing what is selling, what is slipping, and where the next order is hiding, and by the next stop it is gone. Managers coach off gut and stale CRM fields while the sales force that holds every relationship is retiring. Every dropped thread is a lost deal in an industry where the relationship is the asset.

And the pressure is climbing. Private equity is rolling up the industry, and technology is pushing teams to do more with less. Reps have to account for their time, and managers have to show how they are steering the team to sharper execution. Competitive intel can no longer wait for the end-of-quarter debrief, and no manager can explain away an account that went quiet. The same blind spot slows everyone downstream, from procurement to marketing.

AI could close all of this. But knowing how to wire it into a real workflow has opened a chasm between what is possible and what anyone has actually put to work, and it is widest for the thousands of independent distributors up against well-funded retailers like O'Reilly and AutoZone.

An AI-powered workforce is the future of work, and this industry is looking for a partner it trusts to take it there, one that is practical, not theoretical. Tromml has become that voice. Now we are executing on the vision.

Our solution: Minecart

Minecart is an activity intelligence tool that makes sales teams sharper. It gives managers visibility into front-line activity and a way to guide it, and it makes reps more productive: less admin, better-spent time, and a clear next best action that lines the company's priorities up with their own. Everything a rep learns gets captured and fed back, so the next visit starts smarter.

How it works

  • Minecart surfaces the key intel on each account, the issues and the performance shifts, and turns it into a recommended set of priorities the manager uses to guide reps: who to talk to and what to talk about.
  • Reps use that guidance to build their week, weighing performance, initiatives, and at-risk accounts against their own capacity and the most efficient route.
  • Pulling up an account, a rep gets an actionable briefing: ways to build rapport, signals on sales issues and opportunities, campaigns or programs aimed at that account type, and anything left open on the last visit.
  • That frees reps to do what they do best: build the relationship, read the opportunities, and pick up what's happening in the market.
  • After the visit, the rep leaves a voice memo in the Minecart app with everything they just learned.
  • Minecart files the note to the right account, updates the key fields in the CRM, tracks the follow-up, surfaces new opportunities, and merges it with past notes so it's ready for next time.

The name comes from the trommel, the rotating screen miners use to separate valuable ore from waste. We do the same with field data: capture everything, keep the signal, drop the noise.

At its core, Tromml is an AI operating system that guides sales reps to the next best action, so every touch point moves the needle. The longer a team runs it, the harder it is to give up.

For companies with existing CRMs like HubSpot and Salesforce, and BI tools like Tableau serving as their system of record, we plug in and keep the key fields up to date, so the intelligence reaches the rest of the organization to power marketing campaigns and reporting.

A system of action, not another system of record

Our approach starts from one belief: the most valuable intelligence in this industry never gets typed. It gets said out loud: what is selling, what is slipping, which part a shop keeps asking for. Whoever captures it first owns the most valuable dataset in the aftermarket.

Existing tools cannot. Gong and Clari need recorded video calls; our buyer sells thousands of SKUs from the field, over the phone and across the counter. Salesforce and HubSpot need reps to type, and on this front line most never do. The industry pays for systems of record and never gets the record.

Minecart is a system of action. We capture what the field knows, no typing required, turn it into the rep's next best action, and feed the CRM they already bought. A rep covering 200 accounts does not need a cleaner history. They need to know what to do at 8am tomorrow.

That is the investment case: records depreciate, actions compound. Every conversation captured makes the next recommendation smarter, for every rep. What accumulates is front-line demand signal that exists nowhere else, cannot be scraped or bought, and gets more valuable with every day of use. Software gets cheaper. This dataset will not.

Business model and market

Today it is a seat-based subscription that is easy to say yes to: a $500 platform fee plus $100 per user, landing with field sales teams. The expansion is already showing up on its own. Inside sales and customer success run the same process with call recordings as the input, and from there it is every knowledge worker in the building. As agents take on real work, pricing shifts from seats to value: we get paid for what the system does, not who logs in.

The exit

Three kinds of buyers need exactly what we are building, and each is already paying real money for pieces of it.

CRM platforms know their agents are starving for real data. Salesforce acquired Regrello, an agentic-workflow startup, in 2025 specifically to feed Agentforce, and HubSpot Ventures backed Rilla, a field-sales voice capture startup, at a $757M valuation. Both are chasing the same gap Minecart already closes.

Revenue intelligence platforms like Gong are reaching past their own category into enablement and open agent access. What they do not have is a way into physical-goods, outside sales, the world we already own.

Industry incumbents are the clearest case. In their own words they are moving from a system of record to a system of action, and they are opening their platforms to AI agents. But an agent only sees what it is fed, and the incumbents have never had a way to capture what happens on the front line. We won the 2025 MEMA Technology Innovation Award with five people. They know what that is worth.

The comps already show the range: CCC Intelligent Solutions paid $730M for EvolutionIQ, an AI-native claims startup adjacent to our industry, and SAP paid $1.5B for WalkMe to build agent-ready data into its platform. That is the multiple a real data layer commands, in an industry nobody has touched yet. Give the pilots two more years to compound and we are not a feature any of these three buy to check a box. We are the acquisition that decides who wins the category.
