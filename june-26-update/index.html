Aftermarket Managers Now Run on Minecart

This June has been our strongest execution period yet, and we're seeing the benefits of Minecart transform our customers' workflows. Revenue is still heavily in pilots, but demand and conversions are increasing as we design the system of action for aftermarket field teams. That progress shows up in materially improved product-market fit, dramatically increased engineering velocity through AI, and the kind of engagement where customers tell us "please don't take this from me." We have real evidence that Tromml is approaching an inflection point where stronger customer adoption begins translating into meaningful commercial expansion.

More Pipeline, Stronger Relationships

In the aftermarket, relationships are the business. Deals run on who a rep knows and whether they follow through. The problem is that most of what a rep learns in the field, a promise made at the counter, a competitor who just showed up, an account quietly going cold, used to live in their head and die there. Every dropped thread is a deal lost and a relationship that frays.

Minecart fixes that. Reps capture about five times more of what happens in the field and log roughly 30% more activity, because talking into a button beats filling out a CRM. Nothing slips: every promise gets tracked, every account stays warm, and managers can finally see and coach off the real picture. More activity captured and followed through means more pipeline surfaced and more accounts kept, and the relationships the whole industry runs on get deeper instead of leaking away.

We see it in the numbers. Our customer health score went from zero to over 77%, because managers now open Minecart to see what's happening before they do anything else. They don't treat it as a note-taker. They treat it as the place they go to see the truth about their team.

Demand Is Inflecting

What's changed most in the last few weeks is demand, and it's coming to us. Earlier this month I was invited to present at the APSG national meeting, the biggest conference of the year for the distributors we serve.

From there it compounds. One relationship with Capoli opened new deals with Eastern Parts Warehouse and ACI and reinvigorated several other manufacturers. Our workshops closed Snap-on and brought in Dorman, neither from a cold pitch. We aren't chasing any of this. Our reputation pulls it in and our rep-agency partners push it across the line, which is how we grow without a big sales team.

At the top, our biggest opportunity is Advance Auto Parts, where several meetings now have us working toward a pilot next quarter that would secure a spot in their 2027 budget. The value to them is direct: without sunsetting Salesforce or ballooning its cost, we put far more of their people on the data already in it and sharply improve their reporting. New leadership there is driving the project and is aligned with our approach. We're putting the same offer in front of WorldPac, and we're in conversations with several of the country's top distributors, including Fisher Auto Parts.

The Constraint, and How We Attack It

The good problem is that demand is outrunning our capacity to convert it. We won't solve that the old way, by hiring a big team and burning the runway.

We attack it with leverage instead. We rebuilt how we make software around AI: customer meetings turn into feature requests on their own, and our agents plan, code, test, and ship the work. We now ship about six times the product we did at the start of the year with the same headcount, and the same approach is moving into marketing, support, and operations. A company our size can credibly chase Snap-on and Advance and keep pace, which wasn't true a year ago.

Every conversation we capture also makes the moat deeper. It's the whole idea behind our name: a trommel sifts gold from dirt, and a sales call is mostly dirt with a few nuggets that decide the deal. Most enterprise AI fails because the data under it is junk. Ours is captured at the source, in the language of the aftermarket, and kept out of the public models. Minecart gets us in the door, and the intelligence layer underneath is what compounds, the way Verisk's data became the layer insurance runs on. We land, then we expand, the way we laid it out.

What's Left, and the Raise

What's left is conversion and cash. The pilots are live and the demand is real, so we need the runway to turn it into recurring revenue. We're raising a $250,000 bridge to keep us at full speed through the end of the year, and because we run lean on AI, that's enough to keep converting pilots and could get us close to break-even. A larger institutional round follows once more of these pilots sign.

This is a timing challenge, not a question of conviction. Our focus is simple: convert the rep-agency pilots into paying annual customers, get Advance to a paid pilot and a 2027 contract, and keep getting new accounts to value faster. The metrics are still catching up to the progress, but Tromml is a much stronger company than it was a few months ago. If you've been waiting for the inflection before you put more in, this is it, and I'd love to walk you through any of it.
