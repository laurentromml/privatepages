The one line

The problem

In the aftermarket, the most valuable intelligence in a business gets created in conversations between reps and customers. Who is about to churn, where a competitor is winning, which account is ready to grow. Almost none of it makes it back into the company in a usable way. Managers coach off anecdotes. Category teams miss early signals. The knowledge walks out the door when a rep retires. Every system these companies own sits downstream of that lost signal.

What Tromml does

Minecart is the big red button reps talk into. It captures the conversation, ties it to the account, and pulls out the signal: the people, the risks, the opportunities. No manual call reports. Managers finally coach off real activity instead of guesswork. It plugs into the CRM and ERP they already pay for. We are not a rip-and-replace. We are the layer that feeds everything else. This is a system of action, not just more reports.

Why this isn't a note-taker

You will get this faster than most. Note-taking is a commodity. The data underneath it is not. In AI, access to proprietary data like this is not just an advantage, it is a barrier to competition. Every time a rep talks into Minecart and a manager confirms, corrects, or acts on what it surfaced, we capture labeled, vertical-specific signal that no public model and no horizontal tool will ever see: private sales conversations, account dynamics, pricing pressure, the real language of this industry. That data tunes our models for exactly the edge cases the aftermarket hits. The product gets better at this industry specifically, customers embed it deeper, and that generates more signal.

The note-taker is the wedge for adoption. The proprietary data flywheel, and the workflow it owns, is the moat. More than half of AI investors will tell you the rarity of proprietary data is the durable advantage, not the model. That is the asset we are compounding, and it is why we are building this vertical-first and agent-native rather than as one more horizontal wrapper.

How this gets big: land, expand, become the utility

The wedge is the start, not the story.

  • Land. We start with one job that hurts: capturing what reps already know and turning it into structured activity intelligence. Minecart earns its place because it does work no manager can do by hand, reviewing every conversation across the business instead of a handful of ride-alongs.
  • Expand. Once we are the system of record for frontline activity, we add the next products the same customers already need: reporting, opportunity tracking, account intelligence, and agents. This is how the best vertical software companies compound, the layer-cake motion behind Toast, Procore, and ServiceTitan, and it is why a customer's spend with us grows year over year instead of staying flat. We sit on the labor side of the business, not the software-tools line, so the budget we grow into is far bigger than a note-taking subscription.
  • Become the utility. Every conversation we structure builds a private dataset no competitor and no general model can buy or scrape. The more the industry runs on us, the better our models get, and that advantage compounds instead of fading. Over time Tromml becomes the data layer the aftermarket runs its AI on, the same position Verisk holds in insurance and CoStar holds in real estate. Not a vendor anyone can swap out, but the utility the market is wired into.
  • Then repeat in adjacent categories. Tool and hardware distribution, industrial and MRO supply, construction supply, agriculture, and powersports all run the same way: independent distributors, territory reps, and frontline conversations that never reach a system. Tool and hardware alone is a roughly $94B US channel, sold the same relationship-driven way as auto parts, to the same kind of branch buyer, by the same kind of rep. We move category by category because each one shares our buyer, our workflow, and our data model, so the same product earns its place without being rebuilt.

The reason this works now: as AI models become a commodity, the scarce and defensible asset is proprietary data, and the value shifts to whoever owns the data and the rails the agents call. We are building that layer in an industry where we already have the trust to get the data first.

Why now

Margins are tightening, pricing is volatile, and reps are retiring faster than they can be replaced. At the same time, AI went from experiment to expectation in every boardroom in this industry. The companies that structure their frontline intelligence first will be the ones that can actually deploy AI. The rest will keep reacting. There is a narrow window to own that layer, and the door into it is the sales team.

That has roughly six-x'd our engineering velocity without adding headcount, and it is starting to do the same across marketing, support, and operations. It is a big part of how a small team does this much on this little, and that operating leverage compounds into a lasting advantage.
